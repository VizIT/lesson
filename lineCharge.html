Gauss's Law

The Electric Field from an Infinite Line Charge

When we worked with a point charge we recognised spherical symmetry, which inspired us to select a spherical surface to simplify Gauss's law.

Can we find a similar symmetry for an infinite line charge? If we do a little bit of experimenting with the charge and field line diagram, we see that rotation around the axis of the charged line does not change the shape of the field. This leads to a Gaussian surface the curves around the line charge. Since Gauss's law requires a closed surface, the ends of this surface must be closed. That is a cylinder. This symmetry is commonly referred to as cylindrical symmetry.

The field lines are everywhere perpendicular to the wals of the cylinder, and they are evenly distributed around the surface. Just like with the sphere. The ends of the cylinder will be parallel to the elcetric field so that E⋅dA = 0 Once again, Gauss's Law will be easier to evaluate.

Gauss's Law

As always start with Gauss's Law.

∬ S E ⋅ dA = 4πq

We break the surface integral into three parts, the left cap, the body, and the right cap of the cylinder.

∬ S E ⋅ dA = ∬ L E ⋅ dA + ∬ B E ⋅ dA + ∬ R E ⋅ dA

Let's work with the left end cap, L, first.

∬ L E ⋅ dA = ∬ L E cos ⁡ θ dA E is parallel to the cap. = ∬ L E cos ⁡ π2 dA = 0

This makes a great deal of sense. When the field is parallel to the surface the flux through the surface is zero. The process is identical for the right cap, so all the contributions to the flux come from the body of the cylinder.

∬ B E ⋅ dA = ∬ B E cos ⁡ θ dA E is perpendicular to the body. = ∬ B E dA E is constant over the surface. = E ∬ B dA = E A If  L is the length of the cylinder. = E 2πrL Then from Gauss's Law we know: = 4πq If  λ is the charge density of the line, = 4πλL

So that for a line charge:

E=2λr

Then for our configuration, a cylinder with radius r=15.00cm centered around a line with charge density λ=8statCcm.

E=2λr = 28statCcm 15.00cm = 1.07statVcm

For a line charge, we use a cylindrical Gaussian surface. The surface area of the cylinder increases with r, so the field strength decreases with 1r as the field is spread over the surface. If the radius of the Gaussian surface doubles, say from r=5 to r=10 , the surface area, which increases as r, also doubles. The electric field decreases in strength by exactly this factor. We can see it by looking at the increase in space between the field lines where they cross these two different Gaussian surfaces.
